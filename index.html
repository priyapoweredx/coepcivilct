Cement, Aggregates & Water

⚗ Cement Manufacturing
Process: Calcareous (lime) + Siliceous (silica) + Argillaceous (alumina) → burnt at 1300–1500°C in a rotary kiln → Clinkers → Ground with 3–5% Gypsum → Portland Cement. Gypsum controls setting time by retarding C₃A hydration.
Raw Material TypeCompound% in CementRole
CalcareousLime — CaO60–67%Main cementing compound source
SiliceousSilica — SiO₂17–25%Provides strength
ArgillaceousAlumina — Al₂O₃3–8%Controls setting
🧪 Bogue's Compounds (Hydration Products)
Exam trap: C₃A hydrates FIRST (not C₃S). C₃S is responsible for EARLY strength. C₂S for LATE/ultimate strength. C₄AF contributes insignificantly.
CompoundSymbol/Name%Hydration SpeedKey PropertyHeat
Tricalcium SilicateC₃S / Alite25–50%RapidEarly strength, rapid hardeningHigh
Dicalcium SilicateC₂S / Belite20–45%Very SlowUltimate (late) strength, chemical resistanceLow
Tricalcium AluminateC₃A / Celite5–12%First to hydrateFlash setting, max heat of hydrationHighest
Tetracalcium Alumino-ferriteC₄AF / Felite6–12%ModerateInsignificant strength, reduces kiln tempLow
Memory Trick — Bogue's Names
"All Boys Come First"
Alite (C₃S) → Belite (C₂S) → Celite (C₃A) → Felite (C₄AF)
Memory Trick — C₃A Role
"C₃A = Flash & Burn"
First to hydrate, Highest Heat, causes Flash Setting → that's why Gypsum is added to slow it down

🏗 Types of Special Cement
Rapid Hardening Cement (RHC)
Higher C₃S content, finer grinding than OPC. Gains strength much faster. Used in precast elements, cold weather, emergency repairs. NOT the same as Quick Setting Cement.
Quick Setting Cement
Sets very quickly — initial setting in 5 minutes. Achieved by reducing gypsum and adding aluminium sulphate. Used for underwater construction and running water conditions.
Low Heat Cement
Low C₃A and C₃S, high C₂S. Generates minimum heat of hydration. Used for mass concrete — dams, thick foundations. Slow strength gain. IS 12600.
Sulphate Resisting Cement (SRC)
Very low C₃A (<5%). Resists sulphate attack. Used in foundations in sulphate-bearing soils, marine structures, sewage. IS 12330.
Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC)
OPC + pozzolanic material (fly ash IS 1489 or slag IS 455). Lower heat, better long-term durability, cheaper. Widely used in general construction.
White Cement
Made from raw materials with very low iron oxide and manganese. Used for architectural/decorative work, flooring, tile grout.

UNIT 02Fresh Concrete

Workability, Defects & Curing

Every workability test with exact dimensions, defects, and the maturity rule.

⚙ Batching & Mixing
Weigh Batching: Highly recommended · Much more accurate · Used for all quality concrete work
Volume Batching: Less accurate · Used for minor/temporary works only · Not recommended
⚠ Concrete Defects
Segregation
Separation of coarse aggregates from the cement paste/mortar.
Causes
  • Improper mixing (too dry or wet)
  • Dropping concrete from excessive heights
  • Excessive vibration
  • High W/C ratio
Result
Honeycombing — hollow voids/pockets in hardened concrete, severely weakens it.
Bleeding
Excess water rises to the top surface of freshly placed concrete.
Causes
  • High W/C ratio
  • Excess fine particles
Effects
Weakens top layer · promotes scaling/dusting · creates water channels (laitance) that reduce durability
📏 Workability Tests — Exact Numbers Required
Slump Cone Test — Most Common Test
Cone Dimensions
Height = 30 cm · Bottom diameter = 20 cm · Top diameter = 10 cm

Procedure
Filled in 3 equal layers · Each tamped 25 times with a 16 mm diameter rod (tamping rod) · Cone lifted vertically · Slump = height difference

Types of Slump
  • True Slump: Uniform subsidence — ideal, indicates correct workability
  • Shear Slump: One side slides down — indicates low cohesion
  • Collapse Slump: Complete collapse — too wet, too high W/C ratio
Typical Slump Values
  • Very low: 0–25 mm (pavements, foundations)
  • Low: 25–50 mm (lightly reinforced sections)
  • Medium: 50–100 mm (normal RCC)
  • High: 100–175 mm (heavy reinforcement, pumped concrete)
Vee-Bee Consistometer Test
Best for: Low workability (stiff/dry) mixes — where slump cone is not suitable (slump = 0)

Principle: Measures time (seconds) to remold a cone shape into a flat cylinder under vibration

Vee-Bee Time → Workability
  • 0–3 seconds → Very High workability
  • 3–6 seconds → High workability
  • 6–12 seconds → Medium workability
  • 12–20 seconds → Low workability
  • >20 seconds → Very Low workability
Exam shorthand: 5–10 s = High · >20 s = Low
Compaction Factor Test
Formula
CF = W_partial / W_fully compacted
Ratio of partially compacted weight to fully compacted weight of concrete
  • CF > 0.95 → High workability/flowability
  • CF = 0.85 → Medium workability
  • CF < 0.75 → Very low workability
Best for medium to low workability range where slump cone isn't accurate.
🌡 Temperature Effects
High Temperature: Rapid water loss → faster setting → higher early strength BUT lower ultimate long-term strength. Use retarders.
Low Temperature: Slower hydration, freezing risk. Use accelerators (CaCl₂). Concrete should not freeze before gaining strength.
Maturity Rule (Nurse-Saul Equation)
M = Σ (T − T₀) × Δt
M = Maturity (°C·hours) · T = Concrete temperature · T₀ = Datum temperature (usually −10°C) · Δt = Time interval
Key principle: Same maturity = same strength, regardless of how time and temperature are combined.
💦 Methods of Curing
Water Curing

Ponding, sprinkling, wet burlap/hessian cloth. Most effective method. Maintains moisture continuously.

Best method
Membrane Curing

Polythene sheets, curing compounds (resin-based). Prevents moisture evaporation. Used where water is scarce.

Steam Curing

Accelerates strength gain using steam. Used for precast elements. Achieves 28-day strength in hours.

📐 Key Formulas (IS 456-2000)
Flexural Strength (Modulus of Rupture)
f_cr = 0.7 √f_ck
Concrete is weak in tension — only 8–12% of compressive strength. f_ck = characteristic compressive strength (N/mm²)
Modulus of Elasticity
E_c = 5000 √f_ck
Short-term static modulus of elasticity. f_ck in N/mm². Result in N/mm². From IS 456-2000.
🕐 Creep
Definition: Time-dependent permanent strain under sustained/constant load. Concrete keeps deforming even without increasing load. Affected by age at loading, humidity, W/C ratio.
Age at LoadingCreep Coefficient (IS 456-2000)Implication
7 days2.2Highest creep — load applied too early
28 days1.6Moderate creep — standard loading
1 year1.1Least creep — concrete fully matured
Creep Coefficient Trick
7 days → 2.2 · 28 days → 1.6 · 1 year → 1.1
Pattern: Decreases as concrete ages. Older concrete = less creep. Coefficients: 2.2, 1.6, 1.1 → easy to recall as "22, 16, 11" (halving sequence is close)
📉 Shrinkage Types
Plastic Shrinkage

Occurs before setting, when concrete is still plastic. Caused by rapid evaporation of surface moisture. Leads to surface cracks.

Drying Shrinkage

Most significant. Loss of adsorbed water from hardened concrete as it dries. Reversible on rewetting (swelling).

Autogenous Shrinkage

Due to self-desiccation during hydration — water is consumed by cement. Significant in low W/C ratio mixes.

Carbonation Shrinkage

CO₂ reacts with calcium hydroxide → calcium carbonate. Irreversible shrinkage.

Thermal Shrinkage

Contraction as concrete cools after the peak heat of hydration. Major concern in mass concrete.

Remember: 'M' = Mix, number = characteristic compressive strength at 28 days in N/mm²
IS 10262 — Target Mean Strength
f'_ck = f_ck + 1.65 × S
f'_ck = Target mean strength · f_ck = Characteristic strength · S = Standard deviation (from IS 10262 Table 1)
OR f'_ck = f_ck + X (margin), whichever is HIGHER. The 1.65 factor corresponds to 5% acceptable defectives.
📋 IS 10262:2009 Mix Design Steps
Step 1 — Design Stipulations

Grade, workability (slump), exposure condition, max aggregate size, type of cement.

Step 2 — Test Data

Cement grade, specific gravity of materials, aggregate grading, absorption values.

Step 3 — Target Strength

Apply f'_ck = f_ck + 1.65S formula using IS 10262 Table 1 std deviation values.

Step 4 — W/C Ratio

Select from IS 456 Table 5 based on exposure condition (mild, moderate, severe, very severe, extreme).

Step 5 — Water Content

Select from IS 10262 Table 2 based on max aggregate size and required workability/slump.

Step 6 — Cement Content

Cement = Water content ÷ W/C ratio. Check minimum from IS 456 durability requirements.

Step 7 & 8 — Aggregates

Proportion FA and CA using absolute volume method. Adjust for site conditions.

🎯 Statistical Quality Control
Acceptance Criteria (IS 456 Clause 16):
A concrete mix is acceptable if:
(a) The mean strength of any group of 4 consecutive test results ≥ f_ck + 0.825 × std. dev., AND
(b) Any individual test result ≥ f_ck − 3 N/mm²

☢ Chemical Attacks
Sulphate Attack
Source: Sulphate soils, groundwater, seawater
Mechanism: Sulphates react with C₃A and calcium hydroxide → form ettringite (expansive compound, 3CaO·Al₂O₃·3CaSO₄·32H₂O) → expansion → cracking
Prevention: Use Sulphate Resisting Cement (SRC) with low C₃A, low W/C ratio, dense concrete, GGBS
Chloride Attack (Corrosion)
Source: Seawater, de-icing salts, contaminated aggregates, admixtures (CaCl₂)
Mechanism: Chloride ions penetrate concrete → destroy passive oxide film on steel → corrosion → rust expands → spalling of concrete cover
Prevention: Low W/C, adequate cover, epoxy-coated bars, corrosion inhibitors (sodium nitrite), impermeable concrete
Carbonation
Mechanism: CO₂ from atmosphere penetrates concrete → reacts with Ca(OH)₂ → forms CaCO₃ → reduces concrete pH from ~13 to <9 → destroys passive layer on steel → corrosion begins
Prevention: Adequate cover, low permeability, proper curing. Carbonation depth measured by phenolphthalein indicator (turns pink in alkaline, remains colourless in carbonated zone)
Alkali-Silica Reaction (ASR)
Mechanism: Alkalis (Na₂O, K₂O) in cement react with reactive silica in aggregates → form alkali-silica gel → gel absorbs water → swells → causes "map cracking" (crazing)
Prevention: Use non-reactive aggregates, low-alkali cement (<0.6% Na₂O eq), silica fume, fly ash, GGBS (reduce alkali availability)
Acid Attack
Sources: Industrial effluents, acidic groundwater, sewage (H₂SO₄), silage
Mechanism: Acids dissolve calcium compounds in cement paste → surface softening → loss of section
Prevention: Polymer concrete, acid-resistant linings, low permeability concrete
Seawater Attack
Combined action of chloride attack + magnesium sulphate attack. Seawater contains Cl⁻, SO₄²⁻, Mg²⁺, CO₂. Tidal zone is most vulnerable (alternate wetting/drying concentrates salts).
Prevention: SRC, adequate cover (>50mm in splash zone), epoxy coating, silica fume
